Selection Sort

Part 2: Selection Sort Advanced Concepts

Variants & Improvements

  • **Bidirectional Selection Sort:** This is a variant that finds both the minimum and maximum elements in a single pass, then places them at the beginning and end of the unsorted portion of the array.
  • **Stable Selection Sort:** The standard algorithm is not stable. A modified version maintains stability by shifting elements instead of a direct swap, preserving the relative order of equal elements.

Comparison with Bubble & Insertion Sort

  • **Bubble Sort:** Performs many swaps and relatively fewer comparisons. It's efficient for nearly sorted arrays with an early exit optimization.
  • **Insertion Sort:** Also performs many swaps but is highly efficient for nearly sorted arrays and small datasets.
  • **Selection Sort:** Performs the fewest number of swaps but a high number of comparisons. This makes it a good choice when the cost of a swap is much higher than a comparison.

Use Cases in Real World

  • **Small data:** Used in situations where simplicity is more important than efficiency, or when the number of elements is small.
  • **Costly swaps:** Ideal when the elements being sorted are large or complex objects, making the number of swaps a critical performance factor.
  • **Embedded systems:** The in-place nature and minimal memory overhead of selection sort make it suitable for environments with limited resources.

Advanced Exercises

  • Modify Selection Sort to sort an array of strings alphabetically.
  • Implement Bidirectional Selection Sort and compare its time complexity with the normal version.
  • Prove mathematically why Selection Sort always takes $O(n^2)$ comparisons.

Final Thoughts

Selection Sort is not the fastest algorithm, but it’s a fantastic first step into sorting because it builds intuition about comparisons and swaps.

Mastering Selection Sort gives you a strong foundation to understand more efficient algorithms like Merge Sort, Quick Sort, and Heap Sort.
